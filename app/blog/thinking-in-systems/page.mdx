# Thinking in Systems: How I Learn Computer Science

I’m an undergraduate computer science student, and most of my interest in this field comes from *how* things work rather than *what* tools are popular at the moment.

I’m drawn to problems where the answer isn’t immediately clear — where you have to sit with confusion for a while, test assumptions, and slowly build clarity. This page is an attempt to explain how I think about computer science and how I try to learn it.

---

## Why Fundamentals Matter to Me

It’s tempting to rush toward frameworks, libraries, or whatever is trending. I’ve felt that pull too. But over time, I’ve realized that the moments where I actually grow are the ones where I slow down and focus on fundamentals.

Things like:
- Why an algorithm works, not just its final complexity
- What actually happens in memory when a program runs
- Where abstractions leak and why performance degrades

Spending time here can feel inefficient, but it compounds. Once the base is solid, everything else becomes easier to reason about.

---

## Learning Through Discomfort

Most of my learning happens when I’m uncomfortable.

That usually looks like:
- Staring at a bug for far longer than I’d like
- Realizing I don’t understand something I thought I did
- Writing a solution, then throwing it away because I can’t explain it properly

I don’t enjoy being stuck, but I’ve learned not to avoid it. If something feels unclear, I take that as a signal that there’s something worth understanding more deeply.

---

## Competitive Programming (And Why I Don’t See It as the End Goal)

I enjoy competitive programming because it forces precision. You can’t hand-wave details — your logic either holds or it doesn’t.

That said, I don’t see it as the destination. For me, it’s training:
- learning how to break problems down
- recognizing patterns
- building mental stamina for hard problems

What I ultimately care about is applying that thinking to real systems — code that has to survive edge cases, scale, and time.

---

## Systems Thinking Over Surface-Level Solutions

I’m increasingly interested in systems-oriented thinking: understanding how parts interact rather than treating problems in isolation.

This applies whether I’m:
- designing a backend flow
- reasoning about algorithmic trade-offs
- or debugging something that “should” work but doesn’t

I try to ask:
- What assumptions am I making?
- Where could this break?
- What happens when constraints change?

Those questions tend to matter more than the initial solution.

---

## Outside Code (But Not Separate From It)

Outside academics, I spend a lot of time with music, films, and books that reward patience. I think that shapes how I approach software more than I realized at first.

I’m drawn to things that:
- reveal more the longer you sit with them
- value structure and restraint
- don’t try to impress immediately

That mindset carries over into how I like to write and think about code.

---

## What I’m Looking For

I’m interested in environments that care about:
- strong fundamentals over buzzwords
- clarity of thought over speed alone
- long-term growth over short-term output

If you’re more interested in *how* someone thinks than how polished their resume is, we’ll probably get along.

---

### Technologies I love working with

My best friends are C++ and Python while C is a distant one(hehe), libraries like Numpy, Pandas, Sci-kit learn, Tensorflow and Pytorch are my allies and I love working on data processing tasks and anything related to visualisation of huge amounts of data.

I also enjoy working with Operating System concepts and building stuff up from scratch.

Machine Learning and Natural Language Processing in specific are areas I'm deeply passionate about, I love learning about newest advancements in transformers and am always keen to work with them.

--- 

### A Question I Keep Coming Back To

How do you build things that remain understandable — not just correct — as they grow?

That’s something I’m still figuring out.
